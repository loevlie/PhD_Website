Setup: MIL for Brain Scans

A 3D brain scan = a bag of 2D axial slices.

Attention-based MIL learns weights $a_{ij}$ for each slice. These should tell us which slices are important for the prediction of the disease.

Problem: CHIL Harvey et al. showed a simple Gaussian centered on the middle slice outperforms learned attention for localization.
ABMIL (noisy attention)

What Should Attention Look Like?

ABMIL (what we get)

Noisy, unfocused attention

Normal Guidance (ours)

Focused Gaussian around the finding

Label Guidance (oracle)

Uniform over known positives. Not realistic and not truly MIL since it requires instance labels.

How do we encourage the middle option over the top?

Guided Attention

Regularize the learned attention $a_i$ toward a reference distribution $r_i$:

$$\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \ell^{\text{CE}}(y_i, \hat{y}_i)$$
$$\mathcal{L}_{\textcolor{#a6e3a1}{\text{Guided}}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \ell^{\text{CE}}(y_i, \hat{y}_i) \;\textcolor{#a6e3a1}{-\; \frac{\beta}{S_i}\sum_{j=1}^{S_i} r_{ij} \log a_{ij}}$$

The key design choice: what should $r_i$ be?

We use cross-entropy since $a_i$ and $r_i$ are both distributions. Other options to explore: Jensen-Shannon divergence, total variation distance, or MSE.

Label vs Normal Guidance

Figure 1: Instance-level attention for an example test scan.

Label Guidance

$r_i$ = uniform over positive slices. Requires instance labels (upper bound).

Normal Guidance

$r_{ij} \propto \mathcal{N}(j \mid \mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$
Fit from the model's own attention. No extra labels needed.

How Normal Guidance Works

Compute the mean and variance of the learned attention weights, then create a Gaussian reference:

$$\mu_i = \sum_{j=1}^{S_i} j \cdot a_{ij}, \qquad \sigma_i^2 = \sum_{j=1}^{S_i} j^2 \cdot a_{ij} - \mu_i^2$$
$$\hat{r}_{ij} = \mathcal{N}(j \mid \mu_i, \sigma_i^2), \qquad r_{ij} = \frac{\hat{r}_{ij}}{\sum_k \hat{r}_{ik}}$$
Stop-gradient on $r_i$: we don't want the model to minimize the loss by changing the reference distribution.

The self-reinforcing loop: attention focuses → Gaussian tightens → attention focuses more. Is this a feature or a risk?

Open Questions

  • Unimodal assumption: normal guidance fits one Gaussian. What about findings in multiple locations?
  • Classifier guidance: use a per-instance classifier: $\hat{r}_{ij} = \mathbb{1}[c(h_{ij}) > 0.5]$. How well does this work in practice?
  • When to guide?: should we only turn on guidance after ABMIL has trained for a while and has realistic attention values?
  • Upper bound? Is supervised learning always an upper bound on weakly supervised learning?
  • Beyond neuroimaging: does this transfer to computational pathology where there's less spatial structure?

What other reference distributions would make sense?
